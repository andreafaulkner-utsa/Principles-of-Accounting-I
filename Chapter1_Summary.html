üìä Income Statement Deep Dive

The Income Statement shows performance over a period of time (month, quarter, year)

Revenues - Expenses = Net Income

üí∞Revenues (Income)

What the business earns from selling goods/services:

  • Sales Revenue (from selling products)
  • Service Revenue (from providing services)
  • Interest Revenue (from loans made to others)
  • Rental Revenue (from renting property)

üí∏Expenses

Costs incurred to generate revenues:

  • Cost of Goods Sold (cost of products sold)
  • Salaries Expense (employee wages)
  • Rent Expense (office/store rent)
  • Utilities Expense (electricity, water)
  • Advertising Expense (marketing costs)
  • Interest Expense (on borrowed money)

üè¶ Statement of Retained Earnings Deep Dive

Shows changes in retained earnings over a period of time

The Flow:
Beginning Retained Earnings
+ Net Income (from Income Statement)
- Dividends Paid
= Ending Retained Earnings

üìàBeginning Balance

Retained earnings at the start of the period (from previous period's ending balance)

‚ûïAdd: Net Income

Profit earned during the period (comes directly from the Income Statement)

‚ûñSubtract: Dividends

Cash distributions to shareholders (reduces retained earnings)

üéØEnding Balance

Final retained earnings (goes to the Balance Sheet as part of equity)

‚öñÔ∏è Balance Sheet Deep Dive

Shows financial position at a specific point in time (like a photograph)

Assets = Liabilities + Stockholders' Equity
This equation MUST always balance!

üè¢Assets

Resources owned by the business:

  • Current Assets: Cash, Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Prepaid Expenses
  • Long-term Assets: Land, Buildings, Equipment, Vehicles

üí≥Liabilities

Debts owed to others:

  • Current Liabilities: Accounts Payable, Short-term Notes Payable, Accrued Expenses
  • Long-term Liabilities: Mortgages, Long-term Loans, Bonds Payable

üë•Stockholders' Equity

Owners' stake in the business:

  • Common Stock: Investment by shareholders
  • Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits kept in business (from Statement of RE)

üí∞ Statement of Cash Flows Deep Dive

Shows cash receipts and payments over a period of time

üîÑOperating Activities

Day-to-day business operations:

  • Cash IN: Collections from customers, interest received
  • Cash OUT: Payments to suppliers, employee salaries, rent, utilities

Most important section - shows if core business generates cash!

üèóÔ∏èInvesting Activities

Buying/selling long-term assets:

  • Cash OUT: Buying equipment, buildings, land, other companies
  • Cash IN: Selling equipment, buildings, investments

Usually negative for growing companies (investing in future)

üè¶Financing Activities

Getting/repaying money from investors and creditors:

  • Cash IN: Borrowing money, issuing stock to investors
  • Cash OUT: Repaying loans, paying dividends to shareholders

Shows how company finances its operations and growth

üìã Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP)

The "rules of the game" for financial reporting in the United States

Why do we need GAAP?

Without standardized rules, every company could report financial information differently, making comparisons impossible!

üèõÔ∏èFASB

Financial Accounting Standards Board - The primary organization that creates GAAP in the US. Think of them as the "rule makers" for accounting.

‚öñÔ∏èKey GAAP Principles

  • Revenue Recognition: Record revenue when earned, not necessarily when cash is received
  • Matching Principle: Match expenses with the revenues they help generate
  • Full Disclosure: Report all information that could affect decisions

üåçIFRS vs GAAP

International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) are used by most countries worldwide. US uses GAAP, but they're gradually converging.

üîçWho Must Follow GAAP?

  • Public companies: MUST follow GAAP (SEC requires it)
  • Private companies: Often follow GAAP for consistency and credibility
  • CPAs: Must follow GAAP when preparing financial statements

üìö Click to Explore GAAP Assumptions & Constraints

The fundamental assumptions that underlie all of GAAP

Key Assumptions:

Economic Entity: Business activities are separate from personal activities

Going Concern: Business will continue operating in the foreseeable future

Monetary Unit: Only transactions measurable in dollars are recorded

Periodicity: Business life can be divided into artificial time periods for reporting

Key Constraints:

Materiality: Only report items significant enough to affect decisions

Cost-Benefit: Benefits of reporting should exceed the costs

Conservatism: When in doubt, choose the option least likely to overstate assets/income
